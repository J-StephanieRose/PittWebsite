ABOUT

My interest in electromagnetic spectrum began four years ago. At first, I was interested in how emerging technological advancements appeared to be a driving force for policy initiatives. Since then, my focus has shifted towards how technology is being regulated and the policies enforced. This is large in part to the research I am conducting with my advisor, Dr. Martin B.H. Weiss and my fellow colleagues where we are investigating wireless automated enforcement measures â€“ from an incumbent perspective and third party regulators such as the Federal Communications Commission. However, for this work, we are more focused on incumbents and the various types of policy implementation (through de facto and/or de jure rights).

My area of interest within the Department of Informatics and Networked Systems (DINS) is the automation of spectrum policy. Thus far, my perspective on regulator needs within this area is something that is limited in terms of related literature. One of the few things that scholars within my area can agree on is that the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) can/should be doing a better job. Therefore, I decided to use LIS 3600, LIS 3902, and this capstone as a chance to investigate some of these spectrum issues further.

My research for the Digital Studies and Methods certificate, takes a closer look into the Federal Communications Commission's role as a regulator and how they adjudicate enforcement. In order to accomplish this, I have reviewed violations - specifically focusing on spectrum violations as a subset - and used digital methods to analyze this data. This portfolio and its contents are the collective works of my year long journey.

More About This Project

As this is the first phase of this research project, the main questions I wanted to answer were:

  1. How often does radio spectrum interference take place?
  2. Who are the main offenders/violators?
  3. What kind of interference and/or violations related to radio frequency occur?
Although the FCC currently has a map dedicated to enforcement of pirate radio, that is only one type of interference/violation. As radio spectrum moves to become an even more shared environment between commercial and federal users, I hope to find out what (if any) other interference/violations happen within these frequencies in order to better determine how to automate spectrum enforcement.

Since the FCC Enforcement Bureau's database is primarily html files (with all of the document content coded under "pre"), I manually investigated each of the cases (650 so far) for enforcement actions and extracted 217 radio spectrum explicit interference cases. Through the process of coding each case manually, I have realized there are implicit and explicit cases of spectrum interference. Explicit cases include enforcement actions where the frequency band the violator inteterferred with, used illegally, or intentionally blocked signals on are named outright in their case file. There are addiitonal cases that will require more research where the violator has received an enforcement, but their document only states that they interfered with, used, or blocked radio frequencies (sometimes with public safety bands) and no actual frequency band has been documented. Cases of this ilk will be designated as spectrum implicit during the second phase of my research. It appears that this may be a lack of standard (or lack of adhered standard) across the different enforcement bureaus (which cover regions 1, 2, 3 and the FCC Headquarters in D.C.). Each manually coded case for enforcement (whether radio interference explicit/implicit or not) was coded into a spreadsheet. Attributes that I captured included Name of violator, case number (linked to the original html document on the FCC transition.fcc.gov website), date of violation, location, frequency (what frequency they interfered with and/or transmitted on illegally), enforcement type (e.g. warning, notice of violation, penalty (with fine if appropriate), publication (e.g. NOV, FOUO, Consent Decree, Forfeiture, etc.), entity type (e.g. business, individual, duo, religious establishment, or educational institution), enforcement bureau dept region, enforcement bureau department location, and whether or not the violator was a license holder at the time of their offense (which was annotated in their case file).

After collecting the preliminary data (2017 enforcement cases), I reviewed various digital tools in order to find one that would enable me to not only geographically show where radio interference and enforcement are taking place, but also allow me to provide context to each case. Unfortunately, I had to eliminate tools such as ArcGIS, ColorBrewer, and Scribble Maps due to the time constraints, user error, and not really being able to fully provide the details I felt were important to analyze and contextualize the dynamics between interference and enforcement. Eventually, I ended up with a mock up version which was created using Google Maps. By utilizing Google Maps to begin to actualize what data I want to investigate further, I was able to upload and create several layers for different years of enforcement. Since each year of enforcement action varies by color, it is much easier to assess possible trends or "hot spots" for interference.

Furthermore, I completed a brief analysis on the data I have collected thus far. Although these are only initial results, they appear to be showing trends which may lead to additional questions in the second phase and provide a better understanding of the violators/interference cases, but also the manner in which the FCC handles them.

FIRST MILESTONE - LIS 3600 | FALL 2017

When I began this project in 2017, my main question was "is interference a big issue for spectrum enforcement?". My secondary question posited who the main offenders are/were and where these violations/ types of interference typically occur.

During fall 2017, my initial results regarding the data I informed me that only 100 cases out of the data collected were spectrum specific. The main offenders during this first phase were individuals and more often than not, their violation was priate radio - primary location for this type of violation, Florida. At this time, in my research, my points of failure - otherwise termed, limitations - included lack of knowledge regarding GIS tools, time constraints, lack of computer space, and being unfamiliar with the FCC's retention of records.

By spring 2018, my project goals had shifted. Although I was still interested in the primary question - how big of a deal is interference? and my secondary question of what type, who, and where is this interference occurring? - my project goals for the second phase were focused on data collection (and from a more fine-grained view consistency and clarity of data collection) and familiarizing myself with other GIS tools so that my research output would be more than "dots on a map" as Dr. Langmead likes to say.

Now that I am in my third phase of data, as of 08 October 2018; my project goals are to clean the data - as I have collected over 8000 FCC EB records - and continue working familiarizing myslef with other GIS tools in order to fully portray what is occurring within the enforcement sphere regarding spectrum interference violations.

Based on the 2017 data extracted and visualized using Google Fusion Tables, spectrum interference does not appear to be the epidemic one might envision. Main offenders appear to be individuals operating unlicensed operations from residential areas. Additionally, it seems that majority of the offenders are located on the east and west coasts. Majority of the offenses are unlicensed operation notices. Based on the data, violations from businesses and/or major service providers were limited or non-existent.
